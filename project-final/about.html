About Cumaná

Welcome to the oldest city in Venezuela! Discover its rich history, vibrant culture, and stunning landscapes.

History of Cumaná

Cumaná was founded in 1521 by Gonzalo de Ocampo under the name "Nueva Toledo." It is considered one of the oldest European settlements in the Americas. The city played a crucial role during the colonial period as a hub for trade and commerce due to its strategic location on the northeastern coast of Venezuela.

Throughout its history, Cumaná has faced numerous challenges, including pirate attacks, earthquakes, and economic fluctuations. Despite these hardships, the city has preserved its rich cultural heritage and remains a symbol of resilience and tradition.

Culture of Cumaná

Cumaná is known for its vibrant culture, which blends indigenous, African, and Spanish influences. The city is famous for its traditional music, particularly the Gaita Sucrense, a genre that combines lively rhythms with poetic lyrics. During the Christmas season, locals celebrate with gaitas, dances, and colorful parades.

The cuisine of Cumaná reflects its coastal location, with dishes like "cachapa" (corn pancake), "arepa," and fresh seafood being local favorites. Visitors can also enjoy festivals such as the Festival de la Gaita, which showcases the region's musical talent.

Top Places to Visit in Cumaná

Cumaná offers a variety of attractions for visitors, from historical landmarks to natural wonders. Some must-see places include:

  • Castillo de San Antonio de la Eminencia: A historic fortress that once protected the city from pirate invasions.
  • Catedral de Cumaná: One of the oldest cathedrals in Venezuela, showcasing stunning colonial architecture.
  • Playa Colorada: A picturesque beach known for its reddish sand and calm waters.
  • Parque Nacional Mochima: A breathtaking national park featuring turquoise waters, islands, and diverse wildlife.
